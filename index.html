Topic 01 · Physical Chemistry
Water in Food Systems
The most important molecule in food — understanding it explains almost everything else
Why Water is Unique

Water has a bent shape (104.5° bond angle) because the oxygen atom has two lone electron pairs that push the hydrogen atoms together. This shape makes water highly polar — the oxygen end is slightly negative and the hydrogen ends are slightly positive. This polarity is why water molecules stick together through hydrogen bonds. Each water molecule can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds at once, creating a strong, cooperative network.

Unusual Properties (and why they matter in food)
  • High boiling point (100°C) — H-bonds take a lot of energy to break; explains why steaming cooks food
  • High specific heat (4.18 J/g°C) — water resists temperature change; acts as a heat buffer in food
  • High latent heat (2257 J/g) — evaporation removes a lot of heat; key to spray drying and evaporative cooling
  • Ice is less dense than liquid water — unusual! Ice floats, and freezing expands food cells (causing texture damage)
  • Excellent solvent — dissolves salts, sugars, acids easily
Three Types of Water in Food

Bound water: tightly attached to molecules like proteins and starch. It can't be frozen, can't be used by microbes, and doesn't take part in most reactions — it's essentially "locked up." Multilayer water: less tightly held; can dissolve things and participate in some reactions. Free water: behaves like normal water. It can support microbial growth, enzymatic reactions, and chemical spoilage — this is the water that matters most for food safety and shelf life.

Colligative Properties

When you dissolve anything in water, four properties change — and they depend only on how many particles you add, not what they are: (1) Boiling point goes up — adding salt or sugar raises the boiling point of a solution; (2) Freezing point goes down — ice cream doesn't freeze solid because of sugars; (3) Vapour pressure drops — the basis of water activity (Topic 8); (4) Osmotic pressure increases — drives water across membranes; used in jam-making and curing.

Raoult's Law — links to water activity
P = x_w · P°
aw = P / P°
P = vapour pressure of the food; P° = vapour pressure of pure water; x_w = fraction of water molecules in the mixture. Simply put: the more solutes you add, the lower aw drops.
⚠ Exam Tip

Know the difference between bound, multilayer, and free water and why only free water causes spoilage. Make sure you can connect water's unusual properties back to hydrogen bonding. This topic feeds directly into water activity (Topic 8) and glass transition (Topic 5).

Topic 02 · Carbohydrate Chemistry
Carbohydrates
From simple sugars to complex starches — structure drives function
Basic Classification
  • Monosaccharides (single sugars): glucose, fructose, galactose — the building blocks
  • Disaccharides (2 sugars joined): sucrose (table sugar), lactose (milk sugar), maltose (malt sugar)
  • Oligosaccharides: 3–10 units; found in legumes (raffinose, stachyose) — cause flatulence!
  • Polysaccharides: hundreds to thousands of units; include starch, cellulose, pectin, gum
Starch: Two Very Different Components

Amylose: a long, straight chain of glucose units (~20–25% of starch). Think of it like a long rope. Turns blue-black with iodine. Amylopectin: a heavily branched, tree-like structure (~75–80% of starch). Turns red-purple with iodine. Both are made of glucose, but their shape gives them completely different properties in food. Waxy starches (e.g., waxy maize) are nearly 100% amylopectin.

Starch Gelatinisation — What Happens When You Cook Starch

When you heat starch in water (60–80°C depending on the source), the starch granules absorb water, swell up, and lose their ordered crystalline structure. Amylose leaches out into the water. The mixture becomes thick and viscous. When it cools, it sets into a gel. This is how cornflour thickens a sauce, or rice becomes sticky when cooked. It is irreversible — you can't un-cook starch. Measured in the lab using DSC (heat) or RVA (viscosity changes).

Retrogradation — What Happens When Starch Cools and Ages

After gelatinisation, starch chains slowly try to re-organise and re-crystallise during storage. This is called retrogradation. Amylose retrograde quickly (hours) — forms the initial firm gel. Amylopectin retrograde slowly (days to weeks) — this is what makes bread go stale and rice go hard. Retrogradation is reversible by reheating (e.g., day-old bread toasted). Detected by DSC as a melting endotherm around 50–60°C.

Maillard Reaction — Browning with Flavour

This is the reaction that browns bread crusts, roasted coffee, grilled meat, and cookies. It requires two things: a reducing sugar (like glucose) AND an amino group (from amino acids or proteins). The reaction happens in three stages: (1) Sugar + amino acid join up and rearrange (Amadori product); (2) The intermediate breaks down into reactive compounds; (3) These polymerise into brown melanoidins and hundreds of flavour/aroma molecules. It is faster at high temperature, low moisture (aw 0.3–0.7), and alkaline pH.

Caramelisation — Browning Without Protein

Heat sugar by itself to a high enough temperature and it will brown — this is caramelisation. No amino acids needed. Different sugars start caramelising at different temperatures: fructose begins at 110°C, glucose and sucrose at ~160°C, maltose at ~180°C. The products include brown pigments and characteristic caramel flavours (furanones, diacetyl). Used in confectionery, caramel sauce, and crème brûlée.

Key Distinction — Easy to Mix Up!

Maillard reaction: needs a sugar + amino acid/protein. Think bread crust, coffee, meat browning. Caramelisation: only needs sugar + heat. Think toffee, caramel sauce. Both produce brown colours and complex flavours but through completely different chemistry.

Topic 03 · Colloid Science
Gelation
How foods set — networks, junctions, and the science of texture
What is a Gel?

A gel is somewhere between a solid and a liquid. It has a 3D network of molecules that traps water and holds the food in shape — but the network is held together by relatively weak forces, not hard covalent bonds. Think of jelly (gelatin gel), jam (pectin gel), or a boiled egg (protein gel). The key test: a gel's elastic modulus (G') is greater than its viscous modulus (G''), meaning it acts more like a solid than a liquid at rest.

Physical Gels vs Chemical Gels

Physical gels are held together by weak interactions — hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, or ionic bridges. They are usually reversible: melt and re-set with temperature (e.g., gelatin melts in your mouth, agar melts when heated). Chemical gels are held by strong covalent bonds and cannot be reversed (e.g., a cooked egg white — you can't un-cook it). Most gels in food are physical gels.

Gelatin — How it Works

Gelatin comes from collagen (found in animal bones and connective tissue). When dissolved in hot water, the collagen triple helices unwind. On cooling below ~35°C, the chains partially re-form short helical sections called junction zones — these link chains together to make the gel network. This is why gelatin melts at body temperature (melt-in-mouth effect). Bloom number measures gel strength (50–300 g; higher = stronger gel).

Pectin Gels — Two Very Different Types

High-methoxyl (HM) pectin (DE >50%): needs low pH (<3.5) AND lots of sugar (>55°Brix, like jam). The sugar removes water from around the pectin chains and allows them to bond together via hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic forces. Low-methoxyl (LM) pectin (DE <50%): needs calcium ions (Ca²⁺). The calcium bridges neighbouring pectin chains together — this is the egg-box model (Ca²⁺ sits in the cavity between two chains like eggs in a box). No sugar or acid needed.

Agar & Carrageenan

Agar: extracted from red seaweed. Forms a very firm, brittle gel. Sets at ~40°C but doesn't melt until ~85°C — this hysteresis (big gap between setting and melting temperatures) makes agar useful in microbiology plates and hot-climate desserts. κ-Carrageenan: firm, brittle gel; works better with potassium ions. ι-Carrageenan: soft, elastic gel; needs calcium ions. λ-Carrageenan: does not form a gel at all — only thickens.

Protein Gels

Protein gels form when proteins are heated — they unfold (denature), then stick to each other and form a network. The texture depends on conditions: at low ionic strength and away from the protein's pI, proteins form transparent, elastic gels (fine-stranded). Near the isoelectric point or high salt, they form opaque, crumbly gels (particulate). Examples: cooked egg white (ovalbumin), tofu (soy protein), set yoghurt (casein), meat patty (myosin).

How we measure gel strength (Oscillatory Rheology)
tan δ = G'' / G'
G' = elastic (solid-like) modulus; G'' = viscous (liquid-like) modulus. If tan δ < 1, the material is a gel (solid-like). If tan δ > 1, it flows (liquid-like). A firmer gel has a larger G'.

Topic 04 · Protein Chemistry
Proteins in Food Systems
Structure, denaturation, and why proteins behave the way they do in food
The Four Levels of Protein Structure
  • Primary: the sequence of amino acids joined by peptide bonds — like letters spelling a word. Determined by DNA.
  • Secondary: local folding into regular shapes — α-helix (coil held by H-bonds) or β-sheet (flat pleated strands). These are the repeating "shapes" within the chain.
  • Tertiary: the overall 3D shape of the whole protein — how it folds up in space. Stabilised by several interactions (see below).
  • Quaternary: when multiple protein chains combine (e.g., haemoglobin = 4 chains).
What Holds a Protein Together?

Tertiary structure is maintained by: Covalent disulfide bonds (–S–S– bridges between cysteine residues; the strongest). Hydrogen bonds between polar groups. Hydrophobic interactions: non-polar (oily) parts cluster in the protein's core away from water — this is the main driving force for folding. Electrostatic attractions: opposite charges attract (salt bridges). Van der Waals forces: weak but numerous.

Denaturation — Unfolding the Protein

Denaturation means the protein loses its 3D shape — the higher-level structure (secondary, tertiary) is disrupted, but the amino acid chain (primary structure) stays intact. Causes: Heat (most common in food processing), extreme pH, high pressure, agitation (whipping), organic solvents. Usually irreversible in food — a cooked egg white will not become liquid again. Measured by DSC (denaturation temperature Td and enthalpy ΔHd).

Isoelectric Point (pI) — the pH Sweet Spot

Every protein has a pH value at which its positive and negative charges balance out — net charge = 0. This is the isoelectric point (pI). At the pI, proteins are least soluble and most likely to clump together. This is useful: cheesemakers lower pH to ~4.6 to precipitate casein (its pI). Away from the pI, proteins carry a charge and repel each other, so they stay dissolved. Key pI values: casein ~4.6, β-lactoglobulin ~5.2, ovalbumin ~4.7.

Protein Functional Properties
  • 💧 Solubility: affects everything else; drops at pI
  • 💦 Water holding: critical in meat and dairy
  • 🫧 Foaming: proteins stabilise air bubbles (egg white)
  • 🧈 Emulsification: proteins coat oil droplets (mayonnaise)
  • 🧊 Gelation: heat-set networks (egg, tofu, meat)
Disulfide Bonds in Baking

Wheat gluten is made of gliadin + glutenin. During dough mixing, disulfide bonds form and break between cysteine residues, building an extensible, elastic network. This is what gives bread dough its stretch and structure. Additives like SSL and DATEM modify this network — they act on the –SH/–S–S– exchange chemistry.

Topic 05 · Physical State
Glass Transition Temperature (Tg)
Why some foods go soft, sticky, or collapse — it all comes down to molecular mobility
Two States of an Amorphous Food

Some foods (especially dried and frozen ones) exist in an amorphous state — their molecules are disordered, not arranged in a crystal lattice. These materials exist in one of two states separated by the glass transition temperature (Tg): Below Tg → Glassy state: the material is hard, brittle, rigid, and very stable. Molecules barely move. Think of a hard boiled sweet or crispy cracker. Above Tg → Rubbery state: the material becomes soft, sticky, and mobile. Reactions speed up. Think of a stale cracker that's gone soft.

Why Tg Matters in Food
  • Caking of powders: spray-dried powders (e.g., milk powder) clump when stored above Tg because particles become sticky and fuse together
  • Crispness loss: extruded snacks (crisps, crackers) go soft when moisture raises Tg below room temperature
  • Lactose crystallisation: amorphous lactose crystallises above Tg causing graininess in milk powder
  • Freeze-drying: product collapses during drying if temperature exceeds Tg' (the glass transition of the maximally freeze-concentrated solution)
  • Maillard reaction: accelerates dramatically above Tg
Water as a Plasticiser

Water is the most effective plasticiser in food — meaning it lowers Tg. Even a small increase in moisture content can drop Tg significantly. The mechanism: water molecules get between the food polymer chains, increasing the space between them (free volume), which lets the chains move more easily. Example: anhydrous sucrose has Tg ≈ 65°C. At aw = 0.33, Tg drops to ~25°C — now at room temperature, the material is already rubbery and unstable.

The WLF Equation — Predicting Mobility Above Tg

The WLF equation tells us how much faster molecules move (and reactions happen) as temperature rises above Tg. The change is not linear — it's enormous. A small rise in temperature above Tg causes a disproportionately large increase in reaction rates and molecular mobility. This is why the region just above Tg is so critical for food stability. The two universal constants (C₁ = 17.44, C₂ = 51.6 K) apply when Tg is the reference.

WLF Equation (Williams-Landel-Ferry, 1955)
log(aT) = −17.44 × (T − Tg) / (51.6 + T − Tg)
aT = ratio of relaxation times (how much slower reactions/motion are vs. Tg). T = your temperature. Tg = glass transition temperature. A large log(aT) means the system is much more stable than at Tg.
How is Tg Measured?

The standard method is Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC). Unlike melting (which shows a peak), Tg appears as a step change in the baseline — the heat capacity increases as the material transitions from glassy to rubbery. Also measured by Dynamic Mechanical Analysis (DMA) and Thermomechanical Analysis (TMA).

Topic 06 · Sensory Chemistry
Food Sweeteners
How we taste sweetness, and the chemistry behind natural and artificial sweeteners
How Do We Taste Sweetness?

Sweetness is detected on the tongue by a receptor called T1R2/T1R3 — a pair of protein subunits that work together as a lock. Sweet molecules act as the key. When a sweetener binds, the receptor triggers a cascade inside the taste cell: T1R2/T1R3 → α-gustducin → PLC → IP₃ → calcium release → TRPM5 channel opens → depolarisation → sweet signal sent to brain. This is why very different molecules (sucrose, aspartame, steviol) can all taste sweet — they all fit this receptor, just at different sites.

The AH/B/X Theory (older but still useful)

Before receptor biology, Shallenberger & Acree (1967) proposed that any sweet molecule must have: an H-bond donor (AH) and an H-bond acceptor (B) separated by about 2.5–4 Å. Kier later added a hydrophobic region (X). This explains why many structurally different compounds taste sweet — they all share this geometric feature. It's now somewhat superseded by structural biology, but still a good conceptual framework for exams.

When you blend two sweeteners (e.g., acesulfame-K + aspartame, or sucralose + sucrose), the mixture is often sweeter than expected from either ingredient alone. This synergy allows manufacturers to use less total sweetener, reduce cost, and mask unpleasant aftertastes. It's a practical formulation tool, not just theory.

Topic 07 · Biochemistry
Food Enzymes
Biological catalysts — your greatest allies and worst enemies in food processing
Michaelis-Menten Kinetics — The Core Equation
v = Vmax × [S] / (Km + [S])

This describes how enzyme reaction rate (v) changes with substrate concentration [S]. At low [S]: rate is roughly proportional to [S] (lots of empty enzymes). At high [S]: rate plateaus at Vmax (all enzymes are busy). The Km value is the [S] where the rate is exactly half of Vmax — it tells you how much substrate is needed to get the enzyme working at half-capacity. Low Km = high affinity (enzyme works well even at low [S]).

The Lineweaver-Burk Plot

By plotting 1/v vs 1/[S], the Michaelis-Menten curve becomes a straight line. This makes it easy to read off kinetic constants: y-intercept = 1/Vmax; x-intercept = −1/Km; slope = Km/Vmax. Also very useful for identifying the type of inhibition from how the lines shift. In practice, non-linear regression on the original curve is now preferred because the double reciprocal gives unequal weight to errors at low [S].

Three Types of Inhibition
  • Competitive: inhibitor blocks the active site — competes directly with substrate. Km goes up, Vmax unchanged. Can be overcome by adding more substrate. Example: sulfite inhibiting PPO.
  • Non-competitive: inhibitor binds elsewhere (allosteric site), changing the enzyme's shape. Vmax goes down, Km unchanged. Cannot be overcome with more substrate.
  • Uncompetitive: inhibitor only binds when substrate is already attached. Both Km and Vmax decrease. Rare in food systems.
Effect of Temperature and pH

Enzyme activity roughly doubles every 10°C (Q10 ≈ 2) — up to an optimal temperature. Above the optimum, the enzyme denatures and activity drops sharply. pH also has an optimal range: each active site residue needs to be in the right ionisation state. Examples: pepsin works best at pH 2 (stomach acid), salivary amylase at pH 6–7, trypsin at pH 8. In food processing, knowing the enzyme's pH/temp optimum helps you either encourage or inactivate it.

Polyphenol Oxidase (PPO) — Enzymatic Browning

PPO is the enzyme responsible for that brown colour when you cut an apple or potato. It contains copper and needs oxygen. It converts phenolic compounds → o-quinones, which spontaneously polymerise into brown pigments. How to stop it: (1) Blanching (heat inactivates enzyme); (2) Acidification (pH <4 slows it dramatically); (3) Ascorbic acid or sulfite (reduce quinones back before they polymerise); (4) Remove O₂ (modified atmosphere, vacuum).

Key Food Enzymes to Know
  • α-Amylase: breaks down starch → maltose/dextrins; used in bread to improve volume and crust colour
  • Chymosin (rennet): clots milk for cheesemaking by cleaving κ-casein
  • Papain/Bromelain: plant proteases used to tenderise meat
  • Pectinase: clarifies fruit juices by breaking down pectin
  • Transglutaminase: cross-links proteins (Gln–Lys bond); used to make restructured meat and improve dough
  • Lipase: develops flavour in cheese; causes rancidity in cereals if not controlled

Topic 08 · Physical Chemistry
Water Activity (aw)
Not just how much water — but how available it is
What is Water Activity?

Water activity (aw) is a measure of how "available" the water in a food is — not just how much water is present, but how free it is to take part in reactions, support microbes, or escape as vapour. aw = vapour pressure of the food ÷ vapour pressure of pure water, at the same temperature. aw = 1.0 means pure water. aw = 0 means completely dry. Most fresh foods have aw >0.95. The scale runs from 0 to 1 — even small differences matter enormously.

How is aw Measured?

The most accurate method is chilled mirror dew point hygrometry: the food sample is enclosed in a chamber; a mirror is cooled until dew forms; the temperature at which this occurs relates directly to aw. Capacitance sensors (faster, less accurate) are also used in industry. Important: aw is temperature-dependent — always state the measurement temperature. Results given as ERH/100 (equilibrium relative humidity).

The Sorption Isotherm

If you plot aw (x-axis) against moisture content (y-axis) at a fixed temperature, you get an S-shaped (sigmoid Type II) curve called a moisture sorption isotherm. It has three zones: Zone I (aw 0–0.25): tightly bound monolayer water — most stable, Maillard barely occurs; Zone II (aw 0.25–0.7): loosely bound water — solutes dissolve, some reactions start; Zone III (aw >0.7): free water — microbial growth, fast reactions, spoilage. There is also hysteresis — the drying and rewetting curves don't overlap exactly.

BET Equation — Finding the "Ideal" Moisture Content

The BET (Brunauer-Emmett-Teller) equation is used to calculate the monolayer moisture content (m₀) — the amount of water that is tightly bound in one layer to the food surface. This is the moisture content where the food is most stable (minimum reaction rates, no microbial growth). To find m₀, you plot aw/[m(1−aw)] against aw — a straight line gives you m₀ and C (a constant related to binding energy) from the slope and intercept.

⚠ Common Exam Confusion

Moisture content (%) and water activity (aw) are not the same thing. Two foods can have the same moisture content but very different aw values (e.g., sugar vs. salt solutions). aw is the thermodynamically correct measure of water availability. Always use aw for food stability predictions.

Topic 09 · Pigment Chemistry
Food Colour Chemistry
Why foods are coloured, and why those colours change during processing
Chlorophylls — The Green in Plants

Chlorophylls give plants their green colour. There are two types: chlorophyll a (blue-green) and chlorophyll b (yellow-green). Both have a large ring structure with a magnesium ion (Mg²⁺) in the centre — this is the source of the vivid green colour. The problem: when you heat or acidify vegetables, the Mg²⁺ is replaced by hydrogen ions (H⁺), forming pheophytin — which is dull olive/grey-green. This is why overcooked vegetables turn that unappetising grey-green. Preserving the colour requires alkaline conditions or adding copper (as in commercial copper chlorophyllin, E141).

Carotenoids — Yellow, Orange, and Red

Carotenoids are fat-soluble pigments found in carrots, tomatoes, capsicum, salmon, and eggs. They are long molecules with many alternating double bonds (conjugated polyene system) — the more double bonds, the further into the red end of the spectrum they absorb, and the more red/orange they appear. Two main types: Carotenes (no oxygen): β-carotene (orange), lycopene (red). Xanthophylls (with oxygen): lutein (yellow), astaxanthin (salmon/pink). Quite stable to heat and acid, but sensitive to light and oxygen.

Anthocyanins — The pH-Changing Pigments

Anthocyanins create the red, purple, and blue colours in berries, red grapes, red cabbage, and beetroot skin. Their most remarkable feature is that their colour changes with pH: at pH < 3 → bright red (flavylium cation); at pH 4–5 → colourless (carbinol form — colour disappears!); at pH 6–7 → blue/purple (quinonoidal base); at pH > 8 → yellow (chalcone). This is why red cabbage turns bright blue when you add baking soda, and red when you add vinegar.

Myoglobin — Meat Colour

The red colour of fresh meat comes from myoglobin, a protein that holds an iron atom in its centre. The colour depends on the state of that iron: Deoxymyoglobin (Fe²⁺, no oxygen): purple-red — what you see in vacuum-packed meat; Oxymyoglobin (Fe²⁺ + oxygen): bright red — the desirable "fresh" colour seen on the surface; Metmyoglobin (Fe³⁺ — oxidised): brown — the colour of old or oxidised meat. Meat packaging is designed to favour oxymyoglobin (high O₂ MAP keeps meat bright red).

Betalains — Beetroot's Pigments

Betalains are nitrogen-containing pigments found only in certain plants including beetroot, cactus pear, and amaranth. They come in two types: Betacyanins (red-violet): betanin is the most common — this is the red pigment in beetroot. Betaxanthins (yellow-orange): vulgaxanthin. Betalains are used as natural food colourings (E162). They are relatively stable at pH 3–7 but degrade quickly with heat, light, and oxygen. They never co-exist with anthocyanins in the same plant — it's one or the other.

Other Important Colourings
  • Curcumin (E100): yellow pigment from turmeric; turns orange-red above pH 8; light-sensitive
  • Caramel (E150): brown colour made by heating sugars (Maillard or caramelisation); most widely used food colourant
  • Annatto (E160b): yellow-orange from Bixa orellana seeds; used in cheese, margarine
  • Paprika extract (E160c): red-orange carotenoids (capsanthin) from peppers; heat-stable

Topic 10 · Analytical Methods
Colour Measurement
How to objectively measure colour — moving beyond "looks red to me"
Why Measure Colour Objectively?

Human eyes are inconsistent — colour perception changes with lighting, fatigue, and individual differences. The food industry needs numbers to describe colour consistently for quality control, shelf-life studies, and regulatory work. Colour measurement instruments give reproducible numbers that don't depend on who is looking or what light they're standing under. The key system used worldwide is CIELAB (L*a*b*).

CIELAB — The Three Numbers That Describe Any Colour
  • L* (lightness): 0 = perfectly black, 100 = perfectly white. Think of it as the brightness dial.
  • a* (red–green axis): positive a* = reddish, negative a* = greenish. Zero = neutral.
  • b* (yellow–blue axis): positive b* = yellowish, negative b* = bluish. Zero = neutral.
  • Chroma (C*) = √(a*² + b*²): how vivid/saturated the colour is. High chroma = bright, saturated colour.
  • Hue angle (h°) = arctan(b*/a*): 0° = red, 90° = yellow, 180° = green, 270° = blue.
Colour Difference (ΔE*ab) — How Different Are Two Colours?
ΔE*ab = √[ (ΔL*)² + (Δa*)² + (Δb*)² ]
ΔE < 1.0: imperceptible to the human eye | ΔE 1–2: just noticeable | ΔE 2–3.5: clearly perceptible | ΔE > 3.5: obvious colour difference. Used in product quality control — if ΔE is below your threshold, the batch is acceptable.
Instruments Used
  • Colorimeter: simple, inexpensive instrument. Measures L*, a*, b* directly using a few broad filters. Good for routine QC but can't calculate other colour spaces. Think of it as a point-and-click camera.
  • Spectrophotometer: measures the full light spectrum (360–780 nm) reflected or transmitted by the food. More flexible and accurate — can calculate any colour space (CIELAB, Hunter Lab, etc.). Think of it as a full camera with RAW files.
  • Hyperspectral imaging: captures spatial colour variation across the whole food surface — useful for checking uniformity of browning, ripeness mapping.
Measurement Geometry — Important Details

0°/45° geometry: shines light straight on, measures at 45°. Excludes the shiny mirror-like reflection from the surface. Measures colour as it looks to the human eye. Best for most solid foods. Integrating sphere (d/8°): light comes from all directions; can either include (SPIN) or exclude (SPEX) specular reflection. Better for uneven or translucent foods. Always report the geometry, illuminant (usually D65 = daylight), and standard observer when reporting colour data.

Reflectance vs Transmittance

For solid or opaque foods (meat, bread, cheese): measure reflectance — the light bouncing off the surface. For liquids (juice, wine, beer, oil): measure transmittance — the light passing through. Transmittance is often expressed as absorbance using the Beer-Lambert Law: A = εcl, where ε = extinction coefficient, c = concentration, l = path length (cuvette width). This allows quantification of specific pigments at their peak wavelength.

CIEDE2000 (ΔE₀₀) — The Modern Standard

The basic ΔE formula above has a weakness: equal ΔE values in different parts of the CIELAB colour space don't always look equally different to humans. The CIEDE2000 formula adds correction factors for this non-uniformity, especially in the blue region. It is now recommended for any high-precision colour difference work, including correlating instrument measurements with sensory panel results. You don't need to memorise the full formula — but know it exists and why it's better.
